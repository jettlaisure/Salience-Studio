Most product brands approach press coverage backwards. They write a press release about how great their product is and send it to a list of journalists who've never heard of them.

Journalists ignore it. Nothing runs. The brand concludes that press coverage is for brands with bigger budgets or better connections.

That's not the problem. The problem is the approach.

Journalists aren't in the business of covering products. They're in the business of writing stories that their readers find interesting. When you understand that distinction, press coverage becomes much more achievable — and you don't need a PR agency to get it.

**What you'll learn:**
- Why press coverage matters beyond brand awareness (the SEO and AI visibility angle)
- What journalists are actually looking for and why most pitches fail
- How to identify the right publications and the right people within them
- How to craft a pitch that gets read and acted on
- The types of story angles that work for product brands

## Why Press Coverage Matters More Than Most Brands Realize

The obvious benefit: visibility. When a publication covers your product, their audience reads it. Some of them become customers.

But there are two less obvious benefits that are arguably more valuable long-term.

**Backlinks and SEO authority.** When a publication covers your brand and links to your website, that backlink transfers authority. A link from a credible domain tells search engines that your brand has been validated by a trusted source. It raises your domain authority, which makes your product and category pages rank better. This effect compounds: more coverage → more authority → better rankings → more buyers.

**AI model credibility.** AI systems like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Claude build their understanding of brands from what they've encountered across credible sources. A brand mentioned in press coverage — especially in publications with strong editorial credibility — carries far more weight in AI model training and real-time retrieval than a brand that only exists on its own website. Press coverage is third-party validation that AI systems weight heavily when generating product recommendations.

This is why earned media has become a core component of SEO and AI visibility strategy, not just brand marketing. A single editorial mention in a well-regarded publication can benefit your brand's online visibility for years.

## What Journalists Are Actually Looking For

Here's the thing most founders don't want to hear: journalists don't care about your product.

They care about their readers. Their job is to write content that's interesting, useful, or surprising to those readers. Your product is only relevant insofar as it's the most compelling way to tell a story that their readers want to read.

That reframe changes everything about how you approach press.

The question isn't "how do I get coverage for my product?" The question is "what story can I tell that would genuinely interest a journalist's audience — and happens to involve my product?"

A few story types that work for product brands:

*The trend story.* Your product is an example of a broader trend worth covering. "Independent brands are winning back customers from big retailers" — your brand is one of the examples.

*The founder story.* Who built this and why? Unexpected origins, genuine problems solved, or unconventional paths are interesting. Generic "I wanted to build a better product" stories are not.

*The data story.* If you have interesting data from your customers or business — something that reveals a pattern others would find surprising — that's a story. Journalists love original data because it gives them something exclusive.

*The product that solves a real problem.* Not "our product is high quality," but "buyers in this category have been dealing with [specific frustration] for years, and this is why that happens and what we built instead."

*The roundup opportunity.* "Best [product type] for [specific use case]" roundups are published constantly across every niche. Getting into these doesn't require a pitch — it often requires reaching out to the writer or editor who creates them and making a case for why your product belongs.

## Finding the Right Publications and People

The mistake brands make is targeting large, general publications first. A mention in a major outlet is great — but it's also very hard to get without existing relationships or credentials.

Start smaller and more specific. A product brand in the kitchen space should prioritize food and cooking publications, home design publications, and gift guides in that category before targeting general lifestyle media. The audience is more aligned, the competition for space is lower, and a credible mention in a niche publication often carries more SEO value (and more relevant traffic) than a passing mention in a high-traffic generalist site.

When you've identified the right publications, find the specific writers who cover your category. Not the general contact email. The person who writes "best kitchen gadgets" roundups or covers emerging brands in your niche.

Read their last five to ten articles. What do they cover? What angles do they favor? What would genuinely fit their beat? If you can't answer that question, don't pitch yet.

Pitching Tuesday through Thursday, between 9 and 11 AM in the journalist's time zone, is an often-cited best practice — more likely to land in a clean inbox at a moment when the writer is actually considering what to work on next.

## How to Write a Pitch That Gets Read

A press pitch should be short, specific, and about the reader — not about you.

**Subject line.** Specific and honest. Not "EXCLUSIVE: Groundbreaking New Product." Something like "[Story angle] — potential [publication name] piece" or a direct question that captures the story. The goal is to get it opened, not to make promises you can't keep.

**Opening sentence.** Reference their recent work. Show you've read it. One sentence is enough. "Your piece on [specific topic] last month — this is related."

**The story, not the product.** Lead with the angle that serves their audience. Why would their readers care? What's interesting, surprising, or useful here? The product is the vehicle for the story, not the story itself.

**Why now.** Is there a reason this is timely? A trend, an event, a moment in culture that makes this relevant today rather than in six months?

**Brief credentials.** A sentence on the brand: what it is, who it's for, and any social proof (customers, reviews, an interesting origin). Not a pitch deck. Not a press kit. A sentence.

**Easy next step.** "Happy to send samples, hop on a quick call, or share more data if useful." Make it easy to say yes.

Total length: 150–250 words. If it's longer, cut it.

Fifteen personalized pitches to the right journalists consistently outperform thousands of generic press release blasts. The targeting is the work.

## What Makes a Product Brand Pitchable

Before you start pitching, it's worth being honest about whether your brand is ready.

Journalists take risks when they cover an emerging brand. They're vouching for it with their audience. They need confidence that what they write won't embarrass them.

Things that make a product brand pitchable:

*A real product with real customers.* Social proof matters. Reviews, a track record, people who've actually used the product and have something to say about it.

*A clear, defensible positioning.* A brand with a strong point of view is far easier to write about than a brand with no differentiation. The story usually comes from the positioning.

*High-quality visual assets.* Publications need images they can actually run. Professional photography is not optional if you're pursuing editorial coverage. If your product imagery looks like it was shot on a phone in bad lighting, that's a barrier — not a guarantee you'll be passed over, but a barrier worth removing.

*A founder or spokesperson who can speak.* Some of the best coverage comes from journalists quoting the person behind the brand. Being able to give a clear, quotable perspective on your category matters.

If some of these aren't in place yet, that's the work to do before pitching — not after.

## Building Press Coverage Into Your Long-Term Strategy

One mention is nice. A pattern of mentions is a strategy.

The brands that build durable SEO and AI visibility from press coverage are the ones who approach it systematically: identifying target publications, building relationships over time, creating multiple story angles across different moments in the brand's lifecycle.

A product launch is an obvious moment. So is a new partnership, a data release, a seasonal hook, or a notable customer milestone. Each is an opportunity for a story — not a press release, but a real story pitched to the right person.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need a PR agency to get press coverage?

No. A PR agency can scale your outreach and brings existing relationships with journalists — both valuable, especially for higher-volume or time-sensitive campaigns. But the fundamentals — a strong pitch, the right targets, genuine story angles — are achievable without one. Many product brands earn their earliest and most meaningful coverage through direct founder outreach.

### How long does it take to get press coverage?

It varies widely. A well-targeted pitch to a journalist covering your exact niche can result in coverage within a week. Building a pattern of coverage across multiple publications typically takes 3 to 6 months of consistent effort. Relationships with journalists build over time — the first pitch is often the beginning of a longer conversation.

### What's the SEO value of press coverage?

A followed backlink from a credible publication directly increases your domain authority and can meaningfully improve search rankings over time. The SEO value varies by the publication's domain authority and link structure — some publications no-follow links, which reduces direct SEO value but still contributes to brand awareness and AI model training data.

### Should I send free product to journalists?

In many categories, yes — product samples are the norm, and journalists often won't cover a product they haven't tried. Lead with the story angle first. Offer samples as a follow-up, not as the opening move.

### Is press coverage worth it if I'm a small or early-stage brand?

Particularly yes. Press coverage is one of the few marketing channels where small brands compete on story quality rather than budget. A genuinely interesting brand with no marketing spend can earn better coverage than an established brand with no compelling story. The editorial playing field is more level than the paid advertising field.

*Press coverage is earned and inherently unpredictable. Results depend on your story angle, the journalist's interest, editorial calendars, and factors outside your control. A systematic approach improves your odds significantly; guaranteed outcomes are not possible.*
