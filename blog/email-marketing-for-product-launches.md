Most product brands send one email on launch day and wonder why it underperformed.

The email itself is rarely the problem. The lack of a sequence is.

A product launch isn't a moment — it's a window. The brands that maximize it treat the launch as a series of communications that build anticipation before, drive action during, and extend the relationship after. Done right, an email sequence turns a single sales event into the beginning of a customer relationship that keeps paying off.

Here's how to build one.

**What you'll learn:**
- How to structure a pre-launch, launch, and post-purchase email sequence
- What each email should do and what it definitely shouldn't
- Why timing matters more than most brands realize
- How to use email to turn first-time buyers into repeat customers
- The automations that do the heavy lifting once you build them once

## Why Email Beats Every Other Channel for Product Launches

Email is the only marketing channel you fully own. Your Instagram following exists on someone else's platform, subject to algorithm changes and policy shifts that are entirely outside your control. Your ad audience disappears when the budget does.

Your email list is yours. No algorithm decides who sees it. No platform takes a percentage of the outcome. And the intent signal is high — someone gave you their email address because they wanted to hear from you.

Email consistently delivers higher ROI per dollar than paid social for e-commerce brands. Not because it's cheap (though it is), but because people on your email list already have a relationship with your brand. They're not cold leads. They're warm ones.

For product launches specifically, email is the channel that converts warm audiences into buyers. The goal isn't to reach the most people — it's to reach the right people at the right moment in the right way.

## The Pre-Launch Sequence: Building Anticipation

The pre-launch sequence starts 7–10 days before your product goes live. Its job is to prime your audience — build awareness, generate curiosity, and create the conditions for a launch day that sells out rather than trickles.

**Email 1: The announcement (7–10 days out)**

The first email tells your list that something is coming. Not everything — just enough. What's the product? When is it available? Why does it matter?

Keep it short. The point is to plant the flag and give people a reason to stay tuned. Include a way for them to get early access or first notification — a waitlist link, a "reply to this email" prompt, something that lets highly interested buyers signal their intent.

**Email 2: The build (3–5 days out)**

This email goes deeper. More detail on the product: what it is, who it's for, what problem it solves, what makes it worth buying. Show it. If you have photography, use it. If you have testimonials from anyone who's tried it early, this is the place.

This email is also the right place to address the one or two most likely objections. Not defensively — just honestly. "You might be wondering about [thing]. Here's the answer."

**Email 3: Last-chance reminder (1 day out or launch morning)**

A short email. "Tomorrow/Today, [product] goes live." Remind them of any scarcity or limited availability. Remind them of what makes it worth getting. Give them a clear, direct link to where they'll be able to buy.

This email doesn't need to be long. It needs to be timely and clear.

## Launch Day: The Emails That Drive Action

On launch day, you typically want two emails.

**The launch email (launch time)**

This is the moment. Your product is live. The email should be direct, confident, and lead with the thing that matters most to your buyer. Not "We're excited to announce" — that opener doesn't serve the reader. "It's live. Here's what you need to know."

Include: what the product is (assume some people skimmed the pre-launch emails), the price, a clear CTA to the product page, and any relevant urgency — limited quantity, launch pricing, a deadline.

Don't write a novel. The email's job is to get the click.

**The follow-up email (4–8 hours later or next morning)**

Not everyone saw the launch email. Not everyone who saw it clicked immediately. A second launch email — especially for products with limited availability — recovers a meaningful portion of potential conversions.

The tone can be slightly different: "In case you missed it" or "A few things I should mention about [product]." An FAQ format works well here — address the questions buyers are actually asking.

## Post-Purchase: Where Most Brands Leave Money on the Table

The post-purchase sequence is the most underbuilt part of most e-commerce email programs. Most brands send a single order confirmation and call it done.

That's a missed opportunity. The window immediately after purchase is when buyers are most receptive to your brand — they've just made a decision they feel good about. This is the moment to deepen the relationship.

**Order confirmation (immediate)**

This one is functional: order details, expected shipping timeline, contact information if something goes wrong. Make it clear and reassuring. The one thing to add beyond the basics: a brief note that feels human rather than transactional. "You're going to love this" — if you can say it honestly.

**Shipping notification (when it ships)**

Another functional email, but also another touchpoint. Add something small beyond the tracking link: care instructions, a tip for getting the most from the product, a note on what to expect when it arrives.

**The post-delivery check-in (3–5 days after estimated delivery)**

This email does two things: it asks if everything arrived and the buyer is happy, and it plants the seed for a review. Don't demand a review — ask. "If you have a few minutes, a review helps us a lot and helps other buyers know what to expect."

Reviews generated through this email sequence improve your product page SEO, your social proof, and your AI visibility. It's a high-value ask.

**The re-engagement email (2–4 weeks after purchase)**

A short email from your brand — not a push for another purchase, but a genuine check-in. "How are you getting on with [product]?" or a piece of content related to the product category. This keeps your brand in mind without being pushy, and it sets up the next purchase conversation naturally.

## The Automations That Do the Work For You

Everything above can be set up as automated flows triggered by buyer behavior. Once built, they run without manual intervention.

The highest-value automations for product brands:

**Abandoned cart.** A buyer adds a product to their cart and doesn't complete the purchase. An email (or series of two to three emails over 24–48 hours) recovers a significant percentage of these. The first email is gentle — "You left something behind." The second can address a likely objection. The third, if you're using one, can include a small incentive.

**Browse abandonment.** A buyer views a product page multiple times without adding to cart. An email that references the specific product keeps your brand and the product in their consideration set.

**Win-back.** A buyer who hasn't purchased in 60–90 days gets a targeted email to re-engage. This isn't "we miss you" — it's a concrete reason to come back. A new product, a relevant recommendation, something specific.

**Post-purchase cross-sell.** Based on what the buyer purchased, an email recommending a complementary product — sent 2–3 weeks after delivery, after they've had time to actually use what they bought.

These automations are built once and run indefinitely. The ongoing return on the setup cost is significant.

## What to Avoid

A few things that undermine e-commerce email performance:

*Over-emailing.* If your subscribers dread hearing from you, they'll unsubscribe — or worse, stay subscribed but stop opening. Every email should have a reason to exist. "We haven't sent anything in a while" is not a reason.

*Discounting everything.* Training your buyers to wait for a sale is expensive. Use discounts selectively — welcome offers, loyalty rewards, win-back — not as a default mode.

*Ignoring mobile.* Over half of commercial email is opened on a phone. If your email template doesn't render well on mobile, you're losing a large portion of your audience before they even read the subject line.

*Generic subject lines.* "Check out our new product" does not get opened. Specific, direct, and occasionally counterintuitive subject lines do.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many emails is too many for a product launch?

Most brands err on the side of too few, not too many. A 3-email pre-launch sequence, 2 launch day emails, and a solid post-purchase flow is a reasonable foundation. Buyers who are excited about your product won't feel over-emailed. The ones who aren't interested will unsubscribe — which is fine.

### Do I need to discount to drive email conversions?

Not necessarily. Many product brands drive strong launch conversions purely on scarcity (limited availability), urgency (limited launch window), and the quality of the product story. Discounting trains buyers to wait. If your product has a compelling story and clear value, that story is the most powerful conversion tool you have.

### When should I start building my email list?

Before you have a product to sell. An email list built around interest in what you're building is warmer than one built after launch. A simple "notify me when it's available" page is enough. The buyers who sign up before launch convert at significantly higher rates than buyers acquired through ads after.

### What's the right email platform for a product brand?

Klaviyo is the standard for e-commerce because of its native integrations with Shopify and other platforms, and its flow-based automation builder. Omnisend and Drip are credible alternatives. The platform matters less than how well you use it — most e-commerce brands underuse whatever platform they're on.

### How do I grow my email list without running ads?

SEO-driven blog content with email capture, post-purchase opt-ins, giveaways, partnerships with other brands in your space, and referral incentives for existing subscribers are all effective. The highest-quality list growth comes from buyers who opted in because they genuinely want to hear from you — not because they were incentivized with a prize.
